Digital Security Basics

A comprehensive guide to protecting your personal information in the digital world.

Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Reusing passwords is one of the biggest security risks. If one site is breached, attackers can use your credentials on other sites. Instead, use a password manager to generate and store long, random, and unique passwords for every account. A strong password is at least 16 characters and looks like a random string of characters (e.g., `&tJ8#zP!qR5b@9sE`).

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

MFA is your best defense against password theft. Prioritize using an authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or Authy) over SMS-based codes, which can be vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks. For your most critical accounts (like email or finances), consider using a physical security key for the highest level of protection.

Recognize and Report Phishing

Phishing attacks create a false sense of urgency to trick you. Always verify the sender's email addressâ€”attackers often use addresses that are slightly misspelled. Hover over links before clicking to see the actual destination URL. If you receive a suspicious message, do not reply or click. Instead, report it as phishing or junk through your email client.

Keep All Software Updated

Software updates contain critical security patches that fix vulnerabilities discovered by researchers. Delaying updates leaves you exposed to known exploits. Enable automatic updates for your operating system, web browser, and all applications to ensure you are always protected against the latest threats without having to think about it.

Secure and Encrypt Your Devices

Use a strong passcode and enable biometric locks on all devices. Ensure that full-disk encryption is turned on (BitLocker for Windows, FileVault for macOS). This protects your data if your device is physically stolen. Also, set up "Find My Device" features to remotely locate, lock, or wipe your device if it's lost.

Use Public Wi-Fi Safely

Unsecured public Wi-Fi is a hunting ground for attackers who can intercept your traffic. Avoid logging into sensitive accounts. Always use a reputable Virtual Private Network (VPN) on public Wi-Fi. A VPN creates a secure, encrypted tunnel for your data, making it unreadable to anyone snooping on the network.

Review App Permissions

Regularly review the permissions you've granted to apps on your phone and in your browser. Does that game really need access to your contacts and microphone? Revoke any permissions that aren't essential for an app's core function. This minimizes the data that can be collected about you or compromised in a breach.

Back Up Your Data

Regular backups are your safety net against ransomware, hardware failure, or accidental deletion. Follow the 3-2-1 rule: have at least three copies of your data, on two different types of media (e.g., an external hard drive and cloud storage), with one copy stored off-site. Test your backups periodically to ensure they can be restored.
