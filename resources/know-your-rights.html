Guide: Know Your Rights While Protesting

Here is a guide on knowing your rights while protesting, designed to be clear and accessible.

Your Right to Protest

The First Amendment protects your right to assemble and express your views. This applies to public spaces like streets, sidewalks, and parks. While permits may be required for large marches or rallies, you generally don't need one to protest on a public sidewalk.

If Stopped by Police

You have the right to ask "Am I free to leave?" If the officer says yes, you should calmly walk away. You do not have to answer questions about where you are going or what you are doing. You never have to consent to a search of yourself or your belongings.

Right to Remain Silent

The Fifth Amendment protects your right to remain silent. If you are arrested, you should clearly state: "I am going to remain silent. I want a lawyer." Do not say anything else, sign any documents, or make any decisions without a lawyer present.

Photography & Recording

You have the right to photograph or record anything in plain view in a public space, including the police. Police officers may not confiscate or demand to view your images or videos without a warrant. They also cannot delete your recordings.

If You Are Arrested

Do not resist arrest, even if you believe it is unjust. This can lead to additional charges. Remember to invoke your right to remain silent and your right to a lawyer immediately. Try to memorize the badge number of the arresting officer and any important details.

Important Considerations

Non-citizens: You have the same constitutional rights, but an arrest can have severe immigration consequences. Legal Observers: Look for volunteers in bright vests (often green or blue) from groups like the ACLU or National Lawyers Guild; they are there to document police conduct.

Disclaimer:

This information is for educational purposes and is not a substitute for legal advice from a qualified attorney. Rights can vary based on local laws and the specific situation.

KNOW YOUR RIGHTS

A Protester's Pocket Guide

Your Fundamental Rights

  • The First Amendment protects your right to protest in public spaces like parks and sidewalks.
  • You have the right to photograph or record police in public. They cannot take your device or delete your footage without a warrant.

If You Are Stopped by Police

Ask calmly: "Officer, am I free to leave?"

  • If they say YES, walk away slowly and calmly.
  • If they say NO, you are being detained. Do not argue or run.
  • You do NOT have to answer questions about where you are from or what you are doing.
  • You do NOT have to consent to a search of your person or your belongings.

If You Are Arrested

Clearly state these two things and then nothing else:

"I am going to remain silent."

"I want a lawyer."

  • Do NOT resist arrest, even if you feel it is unjust.
  • Do NOT say anything else, sign papers, or make any decisions.

LEGAL SUPPORT / EMERGENCY CONTACT

Write a number to call in case of arrest. Give this number to police if you are arrested.
